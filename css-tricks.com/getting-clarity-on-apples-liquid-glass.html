WWDC Introduction

Always a good idea to start with information straight from the horse‚Äôs mouth.

In short:

  • It‚Äôs the first design system that is universally applied to all of Apple‚Äôs platforms, as opposed to a single platform like Apple‚Äôs last major overhaul, iOS 7.
  • It‚Äôs designed to refract light and dynamically react to user interactions.
  • By ‚Äúdynamic‚Äù we‚Äôre referring to UI elements updating into others as the context changes, such as displaying additional controls. This sounds a lot like the Dynamic Island, supporting shape-shifting animations.
  • There‚Äôs a focus on freeing up space by removing hard rectangular edges, allowing UI elements to become part of the content and respond to context.

Apple also released a more in-depth video aimed at introducing liquid glass to designers and developers.

In short:

  • Liquid glass is an evolution of the ‚Äúaqua‚Äù blue interface from macOS 10, the real-time introduced in iOS 7, the ‚Äúfluidity‚Äù of iOS 10, the flexibility of the Dynamic Island, and the immersive interface of visionOS.
  • It‚Äôs a ‚Äúdigital meta-material‚Äù that dynamically bends and shapes light while moving fluidly like water.
  • It‚Äôs at least partially a response to hardware devices adopting deeper rounded corners.
  • Lensing: Background elements are bended and warped rather than scattering light as it‚Äôs been in previous designs. There‚Äôs gel-like feel to elements.
  • Translucence helps reveal what is underneath a control, such as a progress indicator you can scrub more precisely by seeing what is behind the surface.
  • Controls are persistent between views for establishing a relationship between controls and states. This reminds me of the View Transition API.
  • Elements automatically adapt to light and dark modes.
  • Liquid glass is composed of layers: highlight (light casting and movement), shadow (added depth for separation between foreground and background), and illumination (the flexible properties of the material).
  • It is not meant to be used everywhere but is most effective for the navigation layer. And avoid using glass on glass.
  • There are two variants: regular (most versatile) and clear (does not have adaptive behaviors for allowing content to be more visible below the surface).
  • Glass can be tinted different colors.
