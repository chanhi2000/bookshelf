Postcard From Web Directions Dev Summit, 2025

Author‚Äôs Note

Arrival

The aesthetics of Sydney‚Äôs Museum Station evoke London Underground vibes as my train from Sydney Airport stops under Hyde Park, the oldest public park in Australia and the first to be named after its more famous London counterpart.

Britain‚Äôs on my brain because I want this trip to resemble the Harry Potter stories: the wish-fulfillment narrative of discovering you have special powers and are chosen. In truth, the way I was selected to speak at the Web Directions Dev Summit this year wasn‚Äôt so spontaneous.

The organizer, John Allsopp, recommended my article ‚ÄúHow to Discover a CSS Trick‚Äù on his reading list and connected with me on LinkedIn (meyerlee). Stepping onto the platform like an eleventh-hour rehearsal for the stage, I feel less like the Harry Potter of CSS and more like I‚Äôve signed up to be a novelty museum exhibit. The spooky CSS shopkeeper is a figment of my imagination based on watching too many Simpsons reruns ‚Äî but now I‚Äôve manifested a real-life froghurt situation: a free conference ticket and trip to Sydney in exchange for embarrassing myself in front of the largest audience I‚Äôve ever spoken to.

I procrastinate preparation by sitting down for frozen yoghurt in the Sydney CBD. The froghurt is yummy, but cursed by the cloud of anxiety following me around on this sunny day. So I‚Äôll procrastinate describing my own talk to you by first sharing a few of my favorites from others.

Day One

I‚Äôve arrived and the event kicks off.

Welcome: John Allsopp

The moment John takes the stage, I‚Äôm struck by his showmanship in subverting assumptions about his enthusiasm for tech. He opens by saying he feels ennui with web development, yet hopes the lineup over the next two days might snap him out of his pessimism about the web‚Äôs future.

It‚Äôs the conference equivalent of the literary technique of a frame story: He positions himself as a weary sage who will reappear after each talk for Q&A ‚Äî and yet, as someone who predates PCs, he has greyed like an unavailable option on a computer screen. He says front-end development has reached a ‚Äúlocal maximum,‚Äù borrowing a term from calculus to explain how the tools that got us here have flattened our rate of change. He cites the View Transitions API ‚Äî available as a progressive enhancement since 2023 ‚Äî as an example of a native browser superpower that could subvert the SPA model, yet remains only experimentally supported in React.

‚ÄúThe Browser Strikes Back: Rethinking the Modern Dev Stack‚Äù by Jono Alderson

‚ÄúYou‚Äôre gonna hate me,‚Äù says the keynote speaker Jono Alderson at the top of his talk on rethinking the modern dev stack.

In the break, Jono admits to me that he was nervous about triggering anger by bashing frameworks. It helps that Jono served as frontline fodder, since research shows that everything sounds more credible with a British accent, even if Jono‚Äôs was slightly slurred from jet lag.

Whether he‚Äôs right about nuking frameworks or not, it‚Äôs healthy to reassess whether we need a dependency list longer than our screen port. Even so, I love the idea of web components: transforming the browser into something built for the way we already force it to behave. A decade later, has the situation improved enough to yarn remove React? The answer may go beyond browser capability in 2025. Over coffee, Jono and I discuss how LLMs are trained on oceans of React, reinforcing the assumption that every web app must be an SPA. Escaping React is harder than ever when the future of work is dragging us back into the past (ClaudeAI), much the way recommendation algorithms on social media trap us in our own echo chambers.

‚ÄúIt‚Äôs only gonna get worse,‚Äù says Jono.

And I guess it will, unless we start creating good examples of what browsers can do without dependencies.

It‚Äôs debatable whether you should admit you‚Äôre nervous while giving a talk. Most say you shouldn‚Äôt. The balance I strike is to open with a self-deprecating joke as a way to get the scrolling discussion rolling.

‚ÄúI have a feeling some of you might be scrolling on your devices as we speak, so I urge you to look up ‚Äî and let‚Äôs scroll together for the next half hour.‚Äù

It gets a laugh. It‚Äôs a moment where I translate my CSS-Tricks article style ‚Äî self-referential, breaking the fourth wall ‚Äî into something that works on stage. Happy Hour and Speaker Dinner

Every autistic person should receive a voucher that grants them access to one social situation where people come and talk to them about the thing they are obsessed with. One piece of feedback in particular made me feel seen: Someone tells me a more traditional tutorial would have been fine, but the direction I took was playful, which felt refreshing in a world where discussions of web development can become depressingly utilitarian. Day Two

The Sydney weather cools, mirroring my more chilled mood today. With my presentation behind me, I now walk toward the venue like an anonymous attendee who magically got a free ticket. I brace myself for a morning of AI-heavy talks. My year at work was an AI overdose.

AI is great until there‚Äôs a defect. Then humans blame the AI, and the AI blames humans for misunderstanding its limits.

The novel satirized the Cold War. A Marxist might say intellectual property can‚Äôt exist because creative work is always a product of the zeitgeist. Although the tech that Rupert Manfredi‚Äôs demos blurs the lines of authorship by doing away with discrete apps and websites and composing UIs to meet the user‚Äôs needs on the fly, he is probably not a commie. Rupert‚Äôs talk, ‚ÄúWhat‚Äôs Beyond the Browser,‚Äù is daring. He demos ‚ÄúTelepath,‚Äù a prototype computer with no browser and no apps. ‚ÄúA False Sense of Accessibility: What Automated Testing Tools Are Missing‚Äù by Beau Vass

As I mentioned before, I am autistic. So are my kids. It‚Äôs an invisible disability, and I‚Äôm careful to let the kids know the world won‚Äôt rearrange itself around our autism. Just as you can‚Äôt make something accessible to everyone, you can‚Äôt make the accessible experience the same as everyone else‚Äôs any more than you can make it easy for my son to succeed in a school system that was never designed with neurodiverse people in mind.

In his talk, ‚ÄúWhat Automated Tools Are Missing,‚Äù speaker Beau Vass (beau-vass-0b7b9a3a) demonstrates how automated audits flag non-issues while missing critical failures, sometimes making accessibility worse when followed blindly. The problem isn‚Äôt automated tools themselves; it‚Äôs when passing a Lighthouse audit becomes the goal. Accessibility isn‚Äôt a froghurt topping. It can‚Äôt be added at the end, not even in principle. When we fail, it‚Äôs another reminder that tooling alone won‚Äôt save us.

There aren‚Äôt enough good examples on the web for models to learn from, which means we can‚Äôt expect Claude Code to fix our sites. That said, AI can already simulate how a screen reader user might attempt to complete a task and surface where friction occurs. BrowserStack does this already. Ironically, it may be easier for a machine to put itself in the shoes of a disabled human than for a non-disabled human to do the same, and Beau believes it won‚Äôt be AI that changes the game, but laws and regulations requiring people to care about accessibility. Beau believes it‚Äôs more laws and regulations that will be a game-changer for accessibility than AI.
